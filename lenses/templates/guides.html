Lens Guide

Everything you need to know about choosing the right lenses for your needs

Types of Lenses

Single-Vision Lenses

Single-vision lenses have the same prescription power throughout the entire lens. They're the most common type of lenses and are used to correct:

  • Nearsightedness (myopia)
  • Farsightedness (hyperopia)
  • Astigmatism

Best for: People who need correction for either distance or near vision, but not both.

Progressive Lenses

Progressive lenses provide a smooth transition from distance vision (top) through intermediate vision (middle) to near vision (bottom), without visible lines.

  • No visible lines on the lens
  • Natural vision at all distances
  • Modern alternative to bifocals

Best for: People over 40 who need correction for multiple distances (presbyopia).

Bifocal Lenses

Bifocal lenses have two distinct viewing areas - the top for distance vision and the bottom for near vision, separated by a visible line.

  • Clear separation between zones
  • Easy adaptation
  • Cost-effective option

Best for: People who prefer a clear distinction between distance and reading zones.

Reading Glasses

Reading glasses are designed specifically for near vision tasks like reading, using a computer, or doing close-up work.

  • Optimized for near vision
  • Available in standard powers
  • No prescription required for non-magnifying versions

Best for: People who only need correction for reading and close-up work.

Lens Index Guide

The lens index indicates how thin and light your lenses will be. Higher index = thinner, lighter lenses.

1.50 Standard

Prescription Range: -2.00 to +2.00

Basic plastic lenses. Affordable and suitable for low prescriptions.

1.56 Mid-Index

Prescription Range: -4.00 to +4.00

Thinner than standard lenses. Good balance of cost and performance.

1.59 Polycarbonate

Prescription Range: -6.00 to +6.00

Impact-resistant and lightweight. Excellent for children and sports.

1.67 High-Index

Prescription Range: -7.00 to +7.00

Up to 30% thinner than standard lenses. Ideal for higher prescriptions.

1.74 Ultra-Thin

Prescription Range: -10.00 to +10.00

The thinnest lenses available. Best for very high prescriptions.

Lens Coatings & Add-ons

Anti-Reflection (AR) Coating

Reduces glare and reflections, improving clarity and appearance.

  • Better night vision
  • Reduces eye strain
  • More attractive appearance

Blue Light Protection

Filters harmful blue light from digital screens.

  • Reduces digital eye strain
  • May improve sleep quality
  • Essential for heavy screen users

Photochromic (Transitions)

Lenses that darken in sunlight and clear indoors.

  • Automatic UV protection
  • Convenience of 2-in-1 glasses
  • Various tint options available

UV Protection

Blocks harmful UV rays from the sun.

  • Protects against UV damage
  • Reduces risk of cataracts
  • Essential for outdoor activities

Scratch-Resistant Coating

Makes lenses more durable and long-lasting.

  • Extends lens life
  • Maintains clarity
  • Recommended for all lenses

How to Choose the Right Lens

Step 1: Determine Your Vision Needs

  • Distance only? → Single-vision
  • Reading only? → Reading glasses
  • Both distance and reading? → Progressive or bifocal
  • Computer work? → Consider blue light protection

Step 2: Consider Your Lifestyle

  • Active/Sports? → Polycarbonate (1.59)
  • Outdoor activities? → Photochromic lenses
  • Heavy screen use? → Blue light filter
  • Professional appearance? → AR coating

Step 3: Select Lens Index

  • Low prescription (-2.00 to +2.00)? → 1.50 or 1.56
  • Medium prescription (-4.00 to +4.00)? → 1.56 or 1.59
  • High prescription (-6.00+)? → 1.67 or 1.74

Step 4: Choose Add-ons

  • AR coating (recommended for everyone)
  • Blue light filter (for screen users)
  • Photochromic (for convenience)
  • UV protection (for outdoor activities)

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I update my prescription?

We recommend getting your eyes tested every 1-2 years, or sooner if you notice changes in your vision.

Can I use my old prescription?

Prescriptions are typically valid for 1-2 years. For the best vision correction, use a recent prescription.

What's the difference between AR coating and blue light filter?

AR coating reduces all reflections and glare, while blue light filter specifically blocks blue wavelengths from screens. Both can be combined for maximum benefit.

Are photochromic lenses suitable for driving?

Photochromic lenses may not darken fully inside a car as windshields block UV light. Consider dedicated sunglasses for driving.

How long does it take to adapt to progressive lenses?

Most people adapt within 1-2 weeks. Start by wearing them consistently and moving your head rather than just your eyes when looking at different distances.
